What is Agile?¶

According to the Agile Manifesto, the agile process is all about embracing change through quick turnaround and continuous product delivery, even as the design goals for your project change significantly over the course of its development. Agile developers place a very high value on working software, and while we do value things like documentation and project planning, we ultimately believe that these things are merely a means to an end and are only truly valuable as long as they serve to help us to better develop our software.

See also

The Agile Manifesto [BBvB+01]

The typical method of attaining rapid turnaround when developing a product using agile methodologies is to break the development process down into individual units of work, called “tasks”. In order to identify all of these tasks, it is often easier to start by identifying larger use-cases for your application called “epics”. For example, if you are developing a messaging app, you might consider an epic for adding a search bar to your web UI so that users can more easily search through messages on the platform.

Once you have an epic, you can then further narrow-down the scope of your workload by identifying specific, individual units of functionality called “stories”. An example of a story derived from the above epic could be adding an option to search messages specifically by the “author” field of the message. Once we have a list of stories, we can then break those stories down into individual tasks, like creating an API endpoint for querying the messages database according to the “author” field. Once all of the tasks associated with a story are complete, that story is considered complete. The same is true of epics, with regard to the stories of which they are composed.

Typically, when you given the assignment details of a sprint, you will be provided with a list of epics, perhaps along with a few specific stories, which you will be required to complete before the sprint deadline. Note that you will almost never be given a specific list of tasks. It will be the responsibility of you and your teammates to create your own task list during your planning meeting at the start of each sprint.

What is Scrum?¶

According to the official Scrum Guide, scrum is “a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems”. Essentially, scrum is a specific implementation of the agile method. While other implementations do exist, like kanban and lean manufacturing, this handbook will focus on a scrum-like method.

See also

The Official Scrum Guide [SS20]

The scrum method is a sprint-orient, iterative development process. This means that every 2 weeks or so, your team will be expected to produce a working product, which must include all of the new features that were communicated to you at the beginning of the sprint. It is around the same time that your team will meet to conduct a sprint planning session, during which you will assess all of the epics & stories for the next sprint, and plan out all of the tasks which you will need to complete. Once you’ve planned out your tasks, your team will then go through your sprint plan, and decide who among you will be responsible for each task in the plan.

It’s worth noting that although the agile method we will be using is heavily inspired by scrum, this course is not designed to teach you scrum in its entirety. As a result, we will be completely ignoring many aspects of scrum, like daily stand-ups & team rituals, which are more complex than our use-case requires, and are thus beyond the scope of this course.
